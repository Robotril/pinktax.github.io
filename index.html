The Pink Tax

Gender-Based Price Discrimination: The Cost of Being a Woman

The Numbers: Price Disparities by Sector

Source: Salman, D., & El Ayoubi, S. (2019). Gender-based price discrimination: The cost of being a woman.

1. Personal Care Products

Personal care sees drastic disparities. Women pay significantly more for hygiene products.

  • Hair Care: Shampoo and conditioner show a 48% price difference ($8.39 for women vs. $5.68 for men).
  • Razors: Women pay $8.90 compared to men's $7.99.
  • Body Wash: A 6% price difference exists in body wash products.

2. Adult Clothing

Even for standard basics, the markup exists across the board.

  • Dress Shirts: Women's dress shirts average $58.11, while men's average $51.46 (13% difference).
  • Standard Shirts: A 15% markup is observed for women ($29.23 vs $25.51).
  • Jeans: Women pay approximately 10% more for jeans.

3. Children's Clothing

The Pink Tax starts early, affecting infant and toddler clothing prices.

  • Children's Shirts: Girls' shirts cost $15.82 compared to boys' $13.95.
  • Baby Pants: A 9% difference exists even in infant wear ($18.33 vs $16.77).

4. High-End Fashion

Luxury brands are not immune. Data from Business of Fashion (2016) shows consistent markups for women.

As seen in the chart, brands such as Saint Laurent, Valentino, and Balmain consistently price items marketed to women (pink bars) higher than identical items for men (blue bars).

What is the Pink Tax?

The Pink Tax is defined as a markup on goods and services traditionally marketed towards women. This tax leads women to pay more than men for goods/services that are practically identical or have strong similarities. The name stems from the common notion of associating pink with femininity.

Other names include: "The Gender Tax" or "Gender-based price discrimination."

Historical Background: "Gypped by Gender"

Source: Berliner, M. R. (2020).

One of the earliest recorded cases of the Pink Tax on services came in 1992, when the New York City Department of Consumer Affairs (DCA) conducted a study known as "Gypped by Gender."

Key Findings from 1992:
  • Haircuts: 66% of haircutters charged women 25% more than men for a basic shampoo, cut, and blow dry. These prices were quoted over the phone based solely on gender.
  • Dry Cleaning: Laundering a woman's basic white cotton shirt cost, on average, 27.3% more than a man's shirt.
  • Annual Cost: The DCA calculated that cleaning 3 suits and 10 shirts per month would cost a man ~$466.20 and a woman ~$543.72 annually.

Consumer Power in the Digital Age

Source: Clemons, E. K. (2008).

How Prices Are Set

Pricing is determined in conjunction with the marketplace, competitive offerings, and consumer knowledge. Clemons uses the example of the New York Stock Exchange, where prices are set strictly by market forces (supply and demand).

The Role of Digitization

With the digitization of the contemporary world, marketing no longer solely influences consumer perception of a firm. Instead, consumers rely on:

  • Reviews
  • Online content
  • Word of mouth

Marketplaces today have become "lucid." Optimization of consumption is entirely at the whim of consumers. Firms and consumers do not exist independently, but bounce off one another. Access to information allows consumers to drive the decisions of firms.

Solutions & Safe Harbors

Products Generally Free of the Pink Tax

Not all products are subject to gender-based pricing. The following categories generally maintain price parity:

Essentials
  • Fruits and vegetables
  • Bread, milk, meat
  • Snacks and drinks
Tech & Utilities
  • Phones & Laptops
  • TVs & Headphones
  • Gasoline, Electricity, Water

Consumer Strategies

1. The Mechanic Strategy

Service industries often quote different prices based on customer appearance. To combat this:

  • Get Informed: Research usual pricing online before visiting a mechanic.
  • Call Ahead: Get a quote over the phone before they see you.

2. Negotiation & Segmentation
